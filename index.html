The Marseille Paradox

Europe's primary submarine gateway cannot power its own ambition

Connectivity without precedent

Marseille is the primary European landing point for submarine cables connecting Africa, the Middle East and Asia. The metropolitan area concentrates between 15 and 18 intercontinental cable systems, along with major Internet Exchange Points and over 155 carriers and cloud providers on the main campus.

Power under pressure

Yet installed datacenter capacity remains at ~60 MW. Eight datacenters connected since 2016 hold 800 MW of contractual capacity according to RTE — but actual consumption reached only 120 MW by end-2024. The gap between contracted and consumed megawatts defines this market.

Marseille has emerged as the densest European node for intercontinental submarine cables on the Africa–Middle East–Asia corridor. New systems continue to land: Medusa completed its first shore landing in October 2025, with a first phase announced as ready for service in early 2026. India Europe Xpress is scheduled for Q3 2026 according to TeleGeography. SEA-ME-WE 6 connects Singapore to Marseille via 17 landing points, though no confirmed ready-for-service date had been published as of early 2026.

This connectivity translates into a dense interconnection ecosystem: France-IX and DE-CIX both operate in Marseille, with DE-CIX counting approximately 104 members as of February 2026 according to PeeringDB data. Digital Realty reports over 155 cloud and network providers and 200+ clients present on its campus. For telecom operators, CDNs and content platforms, Marseille offers what few European cities can match: low-latency access to three continents from a single point of presence.

⊕ CVA Point of View

Marseille's connectivity advantage is structural, not cyclical. The landing of three new cable systems in 2025–2027 will further entrench the city's position on intercontinental routes. However, connectivity is a necessary condition — not a sufficient one — for datacenter market growth.

Despite world-class connectivity, Marseille's installed datacenter capacity remains modest: approximately 60 MW live as of early 2025. Paris operates roughly 600 MW — a reflection of its role as France's primary national market, not a benchmark Marseille should be expected to match. The more telling comparison is internal: Marseille's capacity has tripled since 2018, yet remains constrained relative to demand signals. Digital Realty's campus (MRS1–MRS4, estimated at ~46 MW) accounts for over 75% of the installed base and is nearly fully occupied.

The constraint is not absence of demand — it is access to power. Marseille's grid faces competing demands: port decarbonization (with a long-term target of 160 MW of shore power), tramway electrification, urban densification and industrial loads. Enedis launched its "Réseau Marseille" programme in October 2025 — €250 million over 2025–2030 — to strengthen grid resilience. For developers, every megawatt requires a grid allocation strategy.

Marseille appears to be entering a supply boom: over 120 MW of new capacity from major operators is in the pipeline for 2026–2029, potentially tripling the installed base. But headline numbers obscure a critical reality. According to RTE's 2025 network development report, eight datacenters connected since 2016 hold combined contractual capacity of 800 MW — yet actual consumption peaked at just 120 MW by end-2024. This gap reflects projects still under construction, standard over-provisioning, and strategic reservations. RTE notes clients typically consume around 50% of contracted power at steady state — but the 15% aggregate ratio underscores how far the pipeline is from maturity.

Three dynamics drive the mirage. First, over-provisioning: operators contract more than they immediately need, and in some cases reserve grid slots strategically. Second, phasing lag: MRS6 targets first-phase completion in 2028; Telehouse Pennes will take years to fully energize. Third, absorption speed: RTE estimates facilities ramp at ~10% per year, though this varies by segment. The result: Marseille can be oversupplied on paper and undersupplied in practice.

The market's response to the connectivity-power paradox is structural bifurcation. On one side: central Marseille and the port zone, where Digital Realty's campus dominates, grid capacity is saturated, and value is driven by interconnection density. The Métropole's strategic orientations (June 2025) indicate that new hyperscale projects above 12 MW will no longer be approved in the dense urban zone, with future builds directed toward brownfield renewal.

On the other side: the northern periphery — Plan de Campagne, Bouc-Bel-Air, Les Pennes-Mirabeau — where the CRE authorized in December 2025 a 550 MW pooled grid capacity zone. MRS6 features a dedicated 225 kV feed and racks reportedly designed for up to ~120 kW density according to DCD (March 2026). Four projects totalling 300 MW are already contracted according to RTE. These serve a fundamentally different demand profile.

The bifurcation is the market's new architecture — but boundaries are not impermeable. Large peripheral operators could serve smaller retail clients to accelerate absorption. Investors must specify which Marseille they are underwriting. A strategy that conflates the two will underperform in both — but one that ignores the porosity between them will miss emerging dynamics.

The common narrative frames datacenter development as a contest between operators. The reality in Marseille is different: the decisive actors are infrastructure planners. RTE's strategy to channel demand toward Plan de Campagne, CRE's authorization of pooled grid investments, Enedis's €250M programme, and the Métropole's zoning orientations collectively pre-determine where datacenters can and cannot be built — years before the first rack is powered.

At national level, the acceleration is explicit. In January 2026, the French government announced that 63 sites had been identified as favorable for datacenter development, with 26 already secured. This reflects post-AI Summit momentum and France's ambition to position itself for digital infrastructure. Marseille is now operating within a national framework that allocates power capacity as a strategic resource.

The Imperative

Three priorities for Marseille's next chapter

0
the window that will shape the competitive structure for years to come

Marseille stands at an inflection point. Within 36 months, three new cable systems will reach service, over 500 MW of grid capacity will enter development at Plan de Campagne, and the urban core will have locked in its regulatory constraints. The decisions made between 2026 and 2029 will shape the competitive structure of Marseille's datacenter market for years to come.

Priority 1
Reframe the investment lens: from cables to kilowatts
The connectivity thesis is proven and consensual. It no longer differentiates. The differentiating variable is confirmed power access: grid path, substation capacity, connection timeline, cost-share structure. Every investment memo should lead with the power story — not the cable count.
Priority 2
Play the bifurcation, not against it
Urban interconnection sites and peripheral hyperscale campuses serve different customers and carry different risk profiles. Mixing the two dilutes both. The strongest positions will be held by actors who commit to one side of the market — while monitoring the boundary for competitive spillovers.
Priority 3
Treat execution speed as the primary competitive advantage
In a market where contracted capacity can exceed consumption by a factor of six or more, actors who deliver commercially usable capacity within 24 months hold a structural pricing advantage — regardless of scale. Time-to-power is the new time-to-market.
⊕ CVA Point of View

Marseille's paradox is not a weakness — it is a market signal. The gap between connectivity and power creates a scarcity premium for operators who solve both sides of the equation. The next wave of value will be captured not by the largest projects, but by the fastest and best-positioned ones. The race is not for megawatts announced — it is for megawatts delivered.
